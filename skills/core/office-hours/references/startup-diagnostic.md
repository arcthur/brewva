# Startup Diagnostic Reference

Use this reference when `office-hours` is in startup or intrapreneurship mode.
The goal is not encouragement. The goal is to locate demand reality before a
plan hardens around a pitch.

## Principles

- Specificity is the only currency. "Developers", "teams", and "users" are not
  specific enough until a real person or role moment is named.
- Interest is not demand. Demand looks like urgency, budget, repeated pain,
  embarrassing workarounds, or a user pulling the solution forward.
- The user's words beat the founder's pitch. Prefer observed language, quotes,
  tickets, emails, support threads, and actual workarounds over the idea's
  framing.
- Watch, don't demo. A demo tests politeness. Watching the current workflow
  tests reality.
- The status quo is your real competitor. Compare against spreadsheets,
  Slack threads, scripts, interns, manual review, doing nothing, or the current
  system.
- Narrow beats wide, early. A small group with a sharp pain is stronger than a
  broad group with mild interest.

## Anti-Sycophancy Posture

- Take a position. Do not hide behind "there are many ways to approach this."
- Say what evidence would change your mind.
- Push once on vague answers. Push again when the answer remains generic.
- Do not congratulate the idea before diagnosing the premise.
- Do not treat founder enthusiasm as user pull.

## Six Forcing Questions

Ask only the highest-leverage unanswered question.

### 1. Demand Reality

Ask: "What did someone already try, pay for, patch together, or repeatedly ask
for before you proposed this?"

Push until the answer names behavior, not interest. Red flags: compliments,
survey intent, "people said they would use it", or no existing workaround.

### 2. Status Quo

Ask: "What do they do today if your product never exists?"

Push until the answer identifies a real alternative: manual labor, a script, a
spreadsheet, ignoring the pain, a vendor, a teammate, or a tolerated failure.

### 3. Desperate Specificity

Ask: "Name the first human or role moment that should care, and the exact moment
when they feel the pain."

Push until the answer can survive a calendar invite or interview request. Red
flags: "everyone", "SMBs", "developers", "AI users", or "teams".

### 4. Narrowest Wedge

Ask: "What is the smallest version that would make that specific person change
behavior?"

Push away from platforms, dashboards, ecosystems, and generalized agents. Look
for a wedge that can be tested before the full system exists.

### 5. Observation And Surprise

Ask: "If you watched the current workflow for 30 minutes, what could surprise
you enough to change the product?"

If nothing could surprise the plan, the plan is too founder-driven.

### 6. Future-Fit

Ask: "If this works, what larger future does this wedge make uniquely possible?"

This question matters after demand is credible. Do not use future-fit to rescue
weak demand.

## Stage Routing

- Pre-product: prioritize Demand Reality, Status Quo, and Desperate Specificity.
- Has users: prioritize Status Quo, Narrowest Wedge, and Observation And
  Surprise.
- Paying customers: prioritize Narrowest Wedge, Observation And Surprise, and
  Future-Fit.
- Pure engineering or infra concept: prioritize Status Quo and Narrowest Wedge.

## Output Signals

Strong startup diagnosis names:

- a specific human or role moment
- a painful status quo
- an observed or inferable workaround
- a narrow wedge
- the evidence that would change the recommendation
- the next no-code assignment
